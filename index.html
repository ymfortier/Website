Secwépemc’ulucw - My Homeland

Introduction

My name is Yvonne Fortier, a member of the Tk'emlups te Secwépemc, with my roots entrenched in Chu Chua, where I spent my childhood. I am Secwepemc (Shuswap) and my people live within a vast territory in the south central part of the province of British Columbia; approximately 180,000 square km. The landscape is everchanging - mountains, lakes, rivers, creeks, forests, desert lands,and the natural wonders of Mother Nature.

Background

I have travelled to places such as Singapore, Sydney, Manchester, London, Dublin, Los Angelos, Las Vegas, and loved sightseeing and experiencing the different cultures. My favorite trip was to the Grand Canyon, one of the world's 7 wonders. Now I want to explore my homeland - Secwépemc’ulucw, and visit the places my parents and Elders shared in their stories. I acknowledge the people whose footsteps I may follow, and respect all the living beings around me. Welcome to the land of the Secwepemc; usually drummers and singers welcome you to their land with the Welcome Song as you enter their territory.

2. Helmcken Falls

The Helmcken Falls are located within the Simpcw First Nation's traditional territory. The Simpcw inhabit Simpcwulecw, an area now known in English as the North Thompson. Helmcken Falls is the fourth highest waterfall in Canada at 141 m (463 ft).



3. Mount Robson

Mount Robson is the highest point in the Canadian Rockies,and is the second highest peak in B.C., at2,975m (9,760 ft). There are only about 10% of successful climbs to the top of Mount Robson, as there is no easy way to the summit and bad weather can be a deterrent.


4. Radium Hot Springs

There are two large pools, one with a temperature of 39 degrees Celsius, and 29 degrees Celsius. If you can stand the heat, there is a "Plunge Pool", with a temperature of 44 degrees Celsius. Notes to self:

  • Bring Swim Suit

5. Coyote Rock

Located within the Skeetchestn Indian Band's traditional territory. The "Coyote Rock" holds cultural significance and is also known as a balancing rock. In the past similar rocks existed throughout the larger Secwepemc Nation, and it is believed the rocks were put there by Grandfather Coyote to delienate territories.
